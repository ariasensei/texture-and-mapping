📖 Lecture Content

1. Definition of Texture

In 3D graphics, a texture is a 2D image (or procedurally generated pattern) that is applied to the surface of a 3D model to add visual detail without increasing geometric complexity. Think of it as "digital wallpaper" for 3D objects — it defines what a surface looks like: its color, bumpiness, shininess, and transparency.

Real-World Analogy: Imagine you have a plain white ceramic mug. By itself, it's boring — just a smooth white shape. Now imagine wrapping it with a printed vinyl sticker that shows a marble pattern. Suddenly the mug looks like it's made of marble, even though the actual shape hasn't changed. That sticker is essentially a texture. In 3D, we do the same thing digitally — we "wrap" images around models to give them visual identity.

Industry Context: Every surface you see in modern games (Valorant's weapon skins, Minecraft's blocks, GTA V's city streets) and animated films (Encanto's embroidered fabrics, Spider-Verse's halftone dots) relies on textures. Without them, everything would look like untextured gray clay — the way models look in wireframe mode.

2. Texture Resolution

Texture resolution refers to the dimensions of a texture image, measured in pixels (width × height). Common resolutions follow powers of 2: 256×256, 512×512, 1024×1024 (1K), 2048×2048 (2K), 4096×4096 (4K), and 8192×8192 (8K). The "power of 2" rule exists because GPUs process these sizes most efficiently — they're optimized for binary math.

Why Resolution Matters: A low-resolution texture (256×256) stretched across a large wall will appear blurry and pixelated — like viewing a phone photo zoomed in 500%. A high-resolution texture (4K) on that same wall will show crisp brick edges, mortar detail, and subtle color variation. However, higher resolution means larger file size and more GPU memory consumed.

Practical Guidelines: • Mobile games: 512×512 to 1K textures (limited GPU memory) • PC/Console games: 2K to 4K for hero assets, 1K for background props • Film/Cinematics: 4K to 8K (no real-time constraint, renders offline) • VR: 2K minimum per eye — lower causes visible pixels since the screen is inches from your face

Example: In Genshin Impact, the main character's face texture is approximately 2K (2048×2048) for sharp detail during close-up cutscenes, while distant mountain rocks use only 512×512 since players never get close enough to notice.

3. Texture Quality

Texture quality goes beyond just resolution. A 4K texture can still look terrible if it's poorly made. Quality encompasses: sharpness (no unwanted blur), proper tiling (seamless repetition without visible edges), color accuracy (matches the intended material), detail density (appropriate level of detail for the viewing distance), and compression artifacts (minimal JPEG-style blockiness).

The "Texel Density" Concept: Texel density means how many texture pixels (texels) cover each unit of 3D space. Consistent texel density across all objects in a scene ensures nothing looks out of place. If a crate has 512 pixels per meter but the floor has only 64 pixels per meter, the floor will look blurry next to the crate — breaking immersion.

Common Quality Pitfalls: • Stretching — texture pulled across a surface unevenly (looks like melted plastic) • Tiling artifacts — visible repetition pattern when a small texture repeats across a large surface • Color banding — smooth gradients appearing as distinct color steps (usually from over-compression) • Seams — visible lines where UV islands meet on the 3D model

Real Example: Open any AAA game and find a brick wall. Notice how every 5th brick has a slightly different shade, some have chipped corners, mortar varies in width. This "controlled randomness" is what separates professional textures from amateur ones that use identical repeating bricks.

4. Texture Types (PBR Maps)

Modern 3D rendering uses Physically Based Rendering (PBR), which requires multiple texture maps working together to simulate how real materials interact with light. Each map controls a different surface property. Here are the essential types:

🎨 Diffuse / Albedo Map

The base color of a surface without any lighting or shadow information. Pure flat color. A red brick wall's albedo shows brick colors and mortar — but no shadows in the cracks. Think of it as what the surface would look like under perfectly even, shadowless lighting.

🗺️ Normal Map

A purple/blue-tinted image that fakes surface bumps and dents by telling the renderer which direction each pixel "faces." This creates the illusion of depth without adding polygons. The brick cracks appear deep, stones look rough — all from a flat surface with a normal map applied.

✨ Roughness Map

A grayscale image controlling how sharp or blurry reflections are. White = rough/matte (like chalk), Black = smooth/glossy (like a mirror). A kitchen counter might be smooth (dark roughness) while the wooden cutting board beside it is rough (light roughness).

Other Important Map Types

Metallic Map — Grayscale. White = metal, Black = non-metal (dielectric). A sword has white on the blade (metal) and black on the leather grip (non-metal). There's no "semi-metal" in reality — pixels should be near 0 or near 1. Ambient Occlusion (AO) Map — Shows where ambient light is blocked (crevices, corners). Darkens those areas for realistic contact shadows without expensive ray-tracing. Height/Displacement Map — Grayscale image that actually moves geometry up or down. Unlike normal maps (which fake it), displacement maps create real 3D detail — but cost more polygons.

Emissive Map — Defines areas that glow/emit light (neon signs, LED screens, lava). Only the colored areas emit light; black areas emit nothing. Opacity/Alpha Map — Controls transparency. White = fully visible, Black = invisible. Used for leaves, chain-link fences, hair cards — anything with holes or partial transparency.

5. Texture Workflow

A professional texture workflow ensures consistency and efficiency. Whether you're a solo indie developer or part of a 200-person studio, following a structured process prevents wasted time and produces better results:

1

Reference Collection — Gather 10-30 reference photos of the material. Use your phone, Google Images, textures.com, or Poly Haven. Organize references into a mood board (PureRef is free and excellent for this). Study the material: How does light hit it? What imperfections exist? What's the color range?

2

Base Texture Creation — Create or source the base texture. Methods: (a) Photo-based: photograph a real surface, remove lighting with GIMP/Photoshop; (b) Hand-painted: paint from scratch in Krita/Photoshop for stylized looks; (c) Procedural: generate using node-based systems in Blender or Substance Designer for infinite variation.

3

Tiling & Cleanup — Make the texture seamlessly tileable (edges match perfectly when repeated). In GIMP: Filters > Map > Make Seamless. Fix visible seams with the Clone Stamp tool. Test by tiling 3×3 in preview — if you can spot the repeat, fix it.

4

Map Generation — Derive additional PBR maps from your base. Normal maps: generate from height using GIMP's normalmap plugin or Blender. Roughness: desaturate the albedo and adjust levels. AO: bake in Blender using Cycles. Each map must align pixel-perfectly with the albedo.

5

Testing & Iteration — Apply all maps to a test mesh in Blender (use a sphere or the actual game asset). Check under multiple lighting conditions: direct sunlight, overcast, night with point lights. Look for stretching, seam issues, incorrect roughness response. Iterate until the material is convincing from all angles.

🖥️ Laboratory Lesson (3 Hours)

This week's lab covers three hands-on exercises: identifying textures from reference images, comparing resolution quality firsthand, and creating your first simple surface textures using GIMP and Krita. By the end, you'll have a personal Texture Reference Sheet and a Basic Texture Collection.

Part 1: Texture Identification Exercise (45 min)

• Open the provided "Week 2 Texture Samples" folder (downloadable from LMS) • The folder contains 15 unlabeled texture images — a mix of albedo, normal, roughness, height, metallic, and AO maps • For each image, identify the map type and justify your reasoning in 1 sentence • Create a table: File Name | Map Type | Clue Used (e.g., "Purple/blue color = Normal map", "Grayscale with white on edges = AO") • Cross-reference with classmates — discuss any disagreements • Bonus: For 3 textures, identify what real-world material they likely belong to

Part 2: Texture Resolution Comparison (45 min)

• Open GIMP and load a high-resolution texture from textures.com (at least 2K) • Use Image > Scale Image to create versions at: 2048×2048, 1024×1024, 512×512, 256×256, 128×128, 64×64 • Export each as PNG and note file sizes • In Blender: Create 6 planes side by side, apply each resolution version to a different plane • Switch to Material Preview (Z > Material Preview) • Orbit around and zoom in — document at what point pixelation becomes unacceptable • Take comparison screenshots at 3 viewing distances: far (full scene), medium, close-up • Record observations in your lab journal: resolution vs. quality vs. file size tradeoffs

Part 3: Creating Simple Surface Textures (90 min)

• Exercise A — Seamless Tile in GIMP (30 min): Create a new 512×512 image. Paint a simple stone/concrete texture using brushes. Apply Filters > Map > Make Seamless. Test by tiling: Filters > Map > Tile (set to 3×3). Fix any visible seams with Clone Stamp. • Exercise B — Hand-Painted Wood in Krita (30 min): New canvas 1024×1024. Use Krita's textured brushes to paint a wood grain pattern. Study your reference photos. Layer: base brown → dark grain lines → lighter highlights. Keep it tileable by painting from center outward. • Exercise C — Applying in Blender (30 min): Import your stone and wood textures into Blender. Create two objects (cube for crate, plane for floor). Apply textures as Base Color. Generate a basic Normal Map from your textures using Blender's texture node setup. Compare with and without the normal map — screenshot both results.
