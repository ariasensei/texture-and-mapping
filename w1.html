3. Introduction to Texture Mapping

Texture mapping is the process of applying a 2D image (called a texture or texture map) onto the surface of a 3D model. Without textures, all 3D objects would look like smooth, featureless clay. Textures give objects their visual identity — the grain of wood, the roughness of stone, the shine of metal, the pores on human skin.

How does it work? Every 3D model has a UV map — a flattened 2D representation of its surface, like unfolding a cardboard box into a flat sheet. The texture image is then painted or projected onto this flat UV layout. When the model is rendered, the engine wraps the texture back around the 3D shape using the UV coordinates as a guide.

Real-World Analogy: Imagine you want to decorate a basketball. You can't just slap a flat sticker on it — it would wrinkle and stretch. Instead, you'd need to carefully design the graphic knowing it will curve around a sphere. That's essentially what UV mapping solves for 3D artists.

Industry Example: In The Last of Us Part II, every brick wall, rusted pipe, and moss-covered surface you see is a carefully crafted texture mapped onto relatively simple geometry. The textures create the illusion of extreme detail without requiring millions of polygons.

4. Overview of the Texture Pipeline

The texture pipeline is the step-by-step workflow professional studios follow to create textures. Understanding this pipeline from Day 1 helps you organize your work like a professional:

1

Reference Gathering — Collect real-world photos of the material you want to recreate. Example: If texturing a medieval wooden door, photograph 10-20 old wooden doors, noting grain direction, nail rust patterns, and paint peeling.

2

3D Modeling — Create or receive the 3D model that needs texturing. The model must have clean topology (well-organized polygons) for textures to wrap properly.

3

UV Unwrapping — Flatten the 3D model's surface into 2D space. This is like peeling an orange and laying the skin flat. You decide where to place seams (cuts) so they're hidden from the camera.

4

Texture Creation — Paint or generate the actual texture maps (color, roughness, normal, metallic). Tools: Substance Painter for painting directly on the 3D model, or Photoshop/GIMP for flat 2D editing.

5

Integration & Review — Apply textures to the model in the game engine or renderer. Check for stretching, seam visibility, and correct material response under different lighting conditions. Iterate until perfect.

5. Applications of Texture Mapping

🎮 Games

Every surface in modern games uses texture maps. From Fortnite's stylized weapon skins to Elden Ring's weathered stone castles, textures define the visual identity. Game textures must be optimized — small file sizes but high visual impact, because thousands of textures load simultaneously.

🎬 Animation & Film

Pixar and DreamWorks use texture mapping on every character and environment. In movies like Toy Story, Woody's fabric texture, Buzz's plastic sheen, and the carpet fibers are all texture maps. Film textures can be extremely high-resolution (8K+) since they render offline, not in real-time.

🏗️ Visualization

Architects use texture mapping to show clients realistic renders of buildings before construction. Interior designers texture furniture, walls, and floors. Product designers texture prototypes (shoes, electronics, cars) for marketing materials — all before manufacturing a single physical item.

🖥️ Laboratory Lesson (3 Hours)

Today's lab focuses on getting your workstation ready and becoming comfortable with Blender's interface. By the end of this session, you should be able to navigate Blender's 3D viewport confidently and understand where texture-related tools live.

Part 1: Software Installation (45 min)

• Download and install Blender 4.x from blender.org (free) • Download and install GIMP from gimp.org (free Photoshop alternative) • Download and install Krita from krita.org (free digital painting) • Verify all three open correctly • Take a screenshot of each program's splash screen as proof of installation • Organize a dedicated folder: Documents > TextureMapping > Week01

Part 2: Blender Interface Familiarization (75 min)

• Learn the default layout: 3D Viewport, Outliner, Properties panel, Timeline • Practice viewport navigation: Middle-click to orbit, Scroll to zoom, Shift+Middle-click to pan • Switch between Object Mode and Edit Mode (Tab key) • Explore workspace tabs: Layout, Modeling, Sculpting, UV Editing, Texture Paint, Shading • Add a simple cube and apply a basic material (Principled BSDF) • Switch to Material Preview mode (Z key > Material Preview) to see how materials look in real-time • Experiment with changing Base Color — this is your first "texture" interaction!

Part 3: Exploring Material Preview (60 min)

• Open the Shader Editor workspace • Observe the Principled BSDF node — this is the foundation of all PBR materials • Change Base Color to different solid colors and observe the 3D viewport updating • Adjust Roughness slider: 0 = mirror-like, 1 = completely matte. Compare how a metal ball looks at 0.1 vs 0.9 roughness • Adjust Metallic slider: 0 = plastic/wood, 1 = pure metal. Notice how reflections change • Take screenshots showing: (a) Low roughness shiny object, (b) High roughness matte object, (c) Metallic vs non-metallic comparison
